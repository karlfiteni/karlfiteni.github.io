About

I am a postdoctoral researcher in the Dipartimento di Scienza e Alta Tecnologia (DiSAT) at the Università degli Studi dell'Insubria in Como, and a member of the Como Lake centre for AstroPhysics (CLAP), where I work on the dynamics of the Milky Way and other disc galaxies.

At Insubria I am a member of the Galflow group led by Prof. Mattia C. Sormani, where my main project is to understand the structure of the Milky Way's nuclear stellar disc by combining stellar-dynamical models with observational data. I also contribute to the ALMA Central Molecular Zone Exploration Survey (ACES) consortium, which is mapping the molecular gas of the inner Galaxy at unprecedented resolution.

I continue to collaborate with Prof. Victor P. Debattista's group at the University of Central Lancashire, with whom I work on edge modes, spiral structure, and the outskirts of disc galaxies.

I obtained my Ph.D. from the L-Università ta' Malta, supervised by Prof. Joseph Caruana and co-supervised by Prof. Victor P. Debattista. If you are interested in collaborating or in research opportunities in the group, please feel free to get in touch.

Research

My work sits at the interface of galactic dynamics, stellar-dynamical modelling, and galactic archaeology. It currently runs along four threads:

The Milky Way's nuclear stellar disc

My primary line of work, carried out within the Galflow group at Insubria (P.I. Prof. M. C. Sormani), is to understand the structure of the Milky Way's nuclear stellar disc — the kinematically distinct stellar component that occupies the innermost few hundred parsecs of the Galaxy. By combining stellar-dynamical models with observational data, I am developing diagnostics for non-axisymmetric structure in this region (Fiteni et al. 2026, submitted). I also contribute to the ALMA Central Molecular Zone Exploration Survey (ACES), which is mapping the molecular gas of the inner Galaxy at unprecedented resolution.

A 'U-shaped' age profile at the edge of the Milky Way's disc

Featured in Sky & Telescope  ·  Space.com  ·  Universe Today  ·  Phys.org

In Fiteni et al. 2026, A&A, we discovered that the outermost reaches of the Milky Way's star-forming disc display a striking 'U-shaped' stellar age profile: the median stellar age first decreases outward through the disc, and then rises sharply just beyond the disc's break radius. This fossil signature is a direct fingerprint of inside-out disc growth followed by the radial migration of older stars from the inner disc — two long-debated mechanisms of disc-galaxy formation whose joint imprint had not previously been resolved in the Milky Way. The result reframes how we read the outermost regions of the Galaxy as a record of its dynamical history, and provides a sharp test for cosmological simulations of disc-galaxy formation.

Edge modes & spiral structure

A long-running thread of my research is on edge modes — spiral instabilities seeded by sharp features at the boundary of a stellar disc. In collaboration with Prof. Sven De Rijcke (Ghent) and Prof. Victor P. Debattista (UCLan), I use equilibrium stellar-dynamical models combined with linear perturbation theory to show that stellar density breaks are a natural launching site of edge modes, and that they can drive much of the spiral structure observed in disc galaxies (Fiteni et al. 2024, MNRAS).

Bars and clumpy disc evolution

A fourth strand of my work, started during my Ph.D., investigates how internal disc structures drive the secular evolution of disc galaxies. Two of the dominant secular drivers are stellar bars and the massive star-forming clumps seen in high-redshift discs: both can redistribute angular momentum, flip stars onto retrograde orbits, and reshape the chemodynamical structure of the disc.

In Fiteni et al. 2021, MNRAS, we used high-resolution N-body simulations to compare these two mechanisms head-to-head, and found that bars are substantially more efficient than clumps at driving disc stars onto retrograde orbits. The result places quantitative constraints on the relative contributions of bar-driven and clump-driven evolution to the chemodynamical structure of disc galaxies — including the Milky Way's thick disc.
